Why it matters

The Nakamoto coefficient is one of the more honest ways to measure how decentralized a blockchain actually is. In plain terms, it is the smallest number of independent entities that would need to cooperate (or be compromised) to control consensus. A low number means a handful of pools or miners could theoretically call the shots. A higher number means control is spread across many more independent hands, which is closer to the whole point of the exercise.

Chia's original pitch was proof of space and time instead of proof of work, using disk space instead of burning electricity. Whatever "Proof of Space Time 2" ends up meaning technically, and I want to be upfront that the post does not spell out the specifics, the underlying claim is interesting: an update to consensus that could push decentralization metrics in a healthier direction.
