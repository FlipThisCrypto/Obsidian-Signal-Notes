Why it matters

This touches something a lot of builders and everyday users run into: AI models becoming less useful the moment a topic sounds sensitive. Security is one of those areas where being careful makes sense, nobody wants a model handing out real attack instructions to bad actors. But there is a difference between reasonable caution and a model refusing to explain how a firewall rule works, or how to check if a port is exposed, because it pattern matched on a scary word.

When a model gets too cautious, it stops being useful for the people who actually need it most: sysadmins, small dev teams, curious homelab tinkerers, and anyone trying to understand their own systems better. That is a real practical cost, not a hypothetical one.

The practical breakdown

What it points to: a genuine tension in how AI companies tune their models. Too loose, and you get real safety problems. Too tight, and the model becomes annoying or useless for normal security literacy questions.

Who it helps: anyone using AI as a working tool for troubleshooting, learning, or day-to-day security hygiene, not just researchers.

What still needs work: without more detail from the post, I cannot say exactly what Sol and Fable are or how they are built, so take the comparison as one person's real-world impression, not a benchmark.

What I would test first: if I were checking this myself, I would run the same handful of normal security questions (checking open ports, reading a log file, understanding a CVE) across a few different models and see which ones actually help versus which ones freeze up.

What to watch next

I would keep an eye on how different AI providers handle security-adjacent questions over time. This is the kind of thing that tends to shift model to model and update to update, so today's paranoid model could loosen up, and today's relaxed one could get more locked down. Worth watching, not worth overreacting to.
