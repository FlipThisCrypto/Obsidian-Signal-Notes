You Can Just Do Stuff

Originally shared by @YourFriendAndy on X · retrieved 2026-07-18. This is added context and credit, not the original work.

I almost scrolled past this one. Three sentences, no thread, no call to action. I kept coming back to it.

@YourFriendAndy posted a short reminder this week: everything you see in the world was made up by someone. The platforms, the rules, the systems, the products. All invented by people who were figuring it out, and not necessarily people who were smarter or more prepared than anyone else scrolling past the post. The conclusion: you can build things. You can make things happen. And if you choose not to, that part is on you.

That's it. That's the post.

Why it keeps coming back

This is not a new idea, but it is a persistent one. A lot of people working in open source, AI tools, Chia infrastructure, creator projects, and small nonprofits spend a surprising amount of energy waiting for some kind of signal that it is okay to start. The signal does not come. Or it comes too late. Or it gets drowned out by the noise of everything that needs to be done first.

The post names that pattern without being preachy about it. "If you don't, that's on you." That is the part that lands.

The loop is the problem. The arrow is the answer.

What this looks like when you're actually building

Permission anxiety is specific. You wait to ship the tool until the code is cleaner. You wait to post about the project until you have more to show. You wait to ask for feedback until you are less embarrassed by the draft. You wait for someone with credentials to validate the idea before you commit time to it.

None of that waiting makes the thing better. It just delays the only feedback that actually matters, which is what happens when a real thing meets real people.

The practical version of this post: find the smallest buildable unit of whatever is sitting in draft mode and do that today. Not because it will be perfect. Not because it will go viral. Because the feedback from something real is worth more than any amount of private refinement.

This applies to a Chia puzzle, a dashboard for a nonprofit, an automation that saves three hours a week, or a single X post about something you noticed. The permission to start is self-issued or it does not exist.

Rick's take

I keep a list of builders I watch because of what they actually ship, not what they say they are working on. Posts like this one are part of why. @YourFriendAndy is not selling anything here. Just pointing at something true and keeping it short enough to land.

I run into this exact problem all the time in the projects I care about. The Chia tooling that could genuinely help new developers is not waiting for a committee decision. The AI workflows that could help small nonprofits are not waiting for their directors to feel technically ready. The things that move are built by people who started before everything was in order.

That is not a pep talk. It is just what seems to actually happen.

Worth following @YourFriendAndy. Posts like this usually come from someone in the middle of making something, not someone dispensing advice from a distance.
