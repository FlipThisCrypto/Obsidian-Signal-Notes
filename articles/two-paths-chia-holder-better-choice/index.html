Why it matters

The real question underneath this post is not about Chia or Permuto specifically. It is about how you hold genuine conviction without letting the wait corrode you.

This comes up everywhere. Open source infrastructure moves slowly. Nonprofit timelines stretch across years. Long-term positions require patience across cycles that rarely align with anyone's personal schedule. The people making decisions are often working hard inside constraints that are not visible from the outside.

Degen_Waffle seems to see this clearly. Directing resentment toward Bram Cohen and Gene Hoffman over a product approval timeline would be unfair and a waste of good energy. So the question shifts: where does that energy go instead?

The practical breakdown

The answer they landed on is a useful framework for anyone in a similar position.

Going wide does not mean going shallow. Exploring other communities while holding a core conviction is not disloyalty or distraction. It is how you stay engaged with a space without burning out on a single outcome.

Memetic energy is real. The post uses that phrase, and it is worth taking seriously. Enthusiasm, ideas, and credibility move between communities. Someone who has been deep in Chia and then spends time in adjacent ecosystems brings something to share, and often comes back with something useful.

Waiting does not have to mean watching. The time while a product awaits approval can be used to build relationships and stay useful in ways that do not depend on any one event arriving on schedule.
