Why a Blunt List of Business Values Caught My Attention

Originally shared by @alexabelonix on X · retrieved 2026-07-16. This is added context and credit, not the original work.

Most people wrap their ambition in soft language. "Growth mindset." "Synergy." "Building community." So when I saw a post that just said the quiet part out loud, no padding, I stopped scrolling.

What was bookmarked

@alexabelonix posted a short list, basically a call-out for people who share a specific set of business values. The list includes things like monopoly thinking, aggressive marketing, total distribution, category domination, ruthless scaling, hard selling, automating everything, treating a personal brand as a business asset, speed over perfection, profit over hype, and power through owning your own audience. The post cuts off mid-list on "control over," so whatever came next did not make it into what I saw. There are no links attached, no product, no pitch. It reads like someone trying to filter for the kind of people they want to build or work with.

A blunt list of values is really just a filter for who sticks around.

Why it matters

Strip away the harder-edged words like "monopoly" and "ruthless," and a lot of this list is just naming things that creators and small builders already argue about constantly: should you own your audience instead of renting it from a platform, should you ship fast and fix later instead of polishing forever, should your personal brand be treated like a real asset instead of a hobby account. Those are legitimate, ongoing conversations in the creator economy right now, especially as more people realize how fragile it is to build entirely on someone else's platform.

The more aggressive language, monopoly, domination, ruthless scaling, is a different flavor. It is the kind of talk that shows up in certain corners of online business culture where speed and dominance get treated as values in themselves. I do not think that framing is universally true or healthy, but I also do not think the person posting it owes anyone a softer version. It is a filter, not a manifesto.

The practical breakdown

What it does: functions as a self-selecting signal. Anyone who reads that list and nods along is telling @alexabelonix something about how they think. Anyone who winces is also telling them something.

Who it helps: people trying to find collaborators, hires, or an audience that actually matches their working style, instead of spending months figuring that out through trial and error.

Why builders should care: even if the tone is not for everyone, the underlying instinct is useful. Being explicit about your values up front, even blunt ones, saves everyone time. Vague positioning attracts vague people.

What still needs work: the list is a list, not a plan. Words like "monopoly" and "category domination" sound big, but there is no detail here on what that actually looks like in practice, what industry, what product, what audience. Without that, it is more of a mood board than a strategy.

What I would test first: if I were @alexabelonix, I would watch which specific phrase gets the most genuine engagement (not just likes, replies from real people), because that tells you which value is actually doing the filtering work.

Rick's take

I like that this is honest. A lot of people online dress up the exact same instincts, speed, ownership, automation, in softer language because it sounds nicer, and I think that often makes it harder to tell who you are actually dealing with. This list does not do that.

The parts that land for me are the audience ownership piece and speed over perfection. Those match a lot of what I see working for small teams and solo builders right now: own your list, own your channel, ship the imperfect thing and improve it in public. The monopoly and ruthless scaling language is not really my speed, that is more win-at-all-costs framing than builder framing. It reads like a filter for a specific kind of collaborator, and filters do not have to be for everyone.

What to watch next

The interesting thing to watch is what @alexabelonix builds or announces next, since this reads like groundwork for something, maybe a project, a course, a hire, or just a following. A list of values only means something once you see what gets built on top of it. Worth keeping an eye on, without assuming anything about where it lands.
