Three Years of Teaching LP: What One Builder's Quiet Exit Reveals About Crypto Behavior

Originally shared by @toospooky on X · retrieved 2026-07-22. This is added context and credit, not the original work.

@toospooky posted something worth stopping on this week. It was not a breakdown of the next big narrative or a hot take on a project launch. It was an honest accounting of what it cost to spend three-plus years trying to get people to use a tool that would have actually protected them.

What was bookmarked

Originally shared by @toospooky on X, the post is a public reflection on years spent advocating for liquidity provision (LP) as a strategy for token holders. The core argument: instead of just market buying and holding a token you believe in, you can deposit it alongside a paired asset into a liquidity pool on a decentralized exchange. This earns you trading fees, gives the token deeper price support through real liquidity depth, and functions as an automatic, ongoing form of dollar-cost averaging.

After spreading that message consistently for years, @toospooky estimates fewer than 10 percent of the people who saw the advice actually acted on it. Possibly far less. The post closes with a genuine shoutout to the people who did follow through, and steps back from the mission without bitterness.

As relative prices shift, the pool rebalances automatically. You end up with more of whichever token fell.

Why it matters

The pattern @toospooky describes is not a niche problem. It is the default operating mode for most retail participants in crypto markets: find something you believe in, buy it, hold it, and hope.

What is striking is that the tools to do something more sophisticated have existed for years. LP mechanics on decentralized exchanges, and the forks built on top of them across many chains, have been available to ordinary participants for a long time. And yet adoption of LP as a personal strategy, especially among everyday holders rather than professional market makers, has stayed thin.

This is worth sitting with. The infrastructure for more thoughtful participation exists. The behavioral shift has not happened at scale. @toospooky spent years trying to bridge that gap through education and sustained presence, and they estimate the result was under 10 percent adoption among the people they reached. That tells you something honest about where most of the crypto market actually is, regardless of what the technology can do.

The practical breakdown

What LP actually is

When you provide liquidity to a decentralized exchange, you deposit a pair of tokens into a pool. Traders swap through that pool and you earn a share of the trading fees. As the relative price of your two tokens shifts, the pool rebalances automatically, leaving you with more of whichever token fell in relative value.

For a long-term holder of a token they believe in, this has a specific kind of appeal. If your token drops in price, you accumulate more of it through the rebalancing. You are effectively buying the dip automatically while earning fees on the trading volume going through the pool. @toospooky's argument was that this gives holders better entries over time and provides the asset with real price support, rather than purely speculative buying pressure.

What makes it hard to adopt

LP is not complicated to explain briefly, but it carries real complexity underneath. The main risk is impermanent loss: if one of your paired tokens dramatically outperforms the other, the pool's automatic rebalancing can leave you worse off than if you had simply held both tokens separately. Understanding when that risk is acceptable, and when it is not, requires more active thinking than most holders want to do.

The friction is real. The interfaces on most decentralized exchanges are built for people who already understand the mechanics. The mental overhead exceeds the visible reward for most casual participants, even when the underlying math might favor LP over a long enough time horizon.

Who this matters to

People running long positions in tokens they genuinely believe in. Builders thinking about liquidity depth for projects they are involved with. Small operators who want to move past passive holding toward a framework with a built-in risk management layer. And anyone curious about what decentralized exchange tooling was actually designed to enable, beyond just swapping.

Rick's take

What I find genuinely valuable in this post is what @toospooky is not saying. They are not claiming the strategy failed. They are not blaming the community or the markets. They are saying: I gave this a real, sustained effort, it did not move the needle at the scale I hoped for, and I am done chasing that outcome.

That is a specific kind of honesty that is rare in this space.

The shoutout to the people who did learn and act on it stood out to me. @toospooky is right that those holders are working from a more durable framework than most. The ones who learned to think about LP as risk management rather than just yield chasing are ahead in a way that does not always show up in a portfolio screenshot.

I keep wondering whether the tools will eventually close the UX gap enough that this changes. The math has supported this approach for years. The interface just never fully caught up to the learning curve.

What to watch next

Whether LP onboarding improves as decentralized exchange interfaces mature. Whether automated LP management tools emerge that handle enough of the complexity to make this accessible to a broader range of holders. And whether the next wave of chain activity produces the kind of sustained community education that @toospooky was doing manually, but at a larger scale.

The underlying idea is not wrong. The delivery layer is still catching up.
