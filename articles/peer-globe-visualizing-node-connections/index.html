Peer Globe: What It Looks Like When You Finally See Your Node's Connections

Originally shared by @AZurek17 on X · retrieved 2026-07-12. This is added context and credit, not the original work.

I have stared at a peer list before. A column of IP addresses sitting in a terminal window, technically meaningful, completely unmoving. You know your node is connected to other nodes. You know that in theory. But knowing something and seeing it are not the same thing, and that gap is exactly what stopped me on this bookmark.

What was bookmarked

@AZurek17 posted the first half of a two-part share about something called Peer Globe. The point of the post is simple and honest: his node has always been connected to peers, but a list of IP addresses never made that connection feel real. Peer Globe is the tool that changed that for him, turning the abstract fact of 'my node has peers' into something he can actually look at. The post links to an image of the result rather than a project page, so the details of how Peer Globe works under the hood are not spelled out yet. This is part one of two, so there is more coming from him on this.

The same information, two ways: a list nobody feels, and a globe that makes the network real.

Why it matters

A lot of the infrastructure that makes peer-to-peer networks work is invisible by design. That is kind of the point of decentralization, nobody needs a control room with blinking lights to make the network function. But invisible also means most people running a node never actually experience what they are participating in. They trust the number in the peer count field and move on.

Tools like Peer Globe sit in that gap between running infrastructure and understanding it. This is the same instinct behind dashboards, block explorers, and any visualization that takes something technically true and makes it emotionally true. For node operators, hobbyists, and anyone trying to get more people comfortable running their own infrastructure, that shift matters more than it sounds like it should.

The practical breakdown

What it does: takes a node's peer connection list, which is normally just IP addresses, and renders it as a visual, apparently something like a globe showing where those connections are actually located.

Who it helps: anyone running a node who wants a gut-level sense of their place in the network, not just a technical one. Also useful for teaching. A globe with live connection lines communicates 'this network is real and distributed' faster than any paragraph of explanation.

What problem it solves: the disconnect between operating infrastructure and understanding it. Most node software gives you logs and counts. Very little of it gives you a feeling.

What still needs work, or what is still unknown: we only have half the story here. Part one shows the reaction, not the mechanics. It is not clear yet whether Peer Globe is a personal script, a public tool, or something meant to be shared more widely. That is exactly what part two should clarify.

What I would test first: if this becomes something others can run, I would want to see it against a node with a lot of peer churn, to see whether the visualization stays readable when connections are constantly dropping and reconnecting, which is normal and healthy for a real network.
